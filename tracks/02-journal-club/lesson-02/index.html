Lesson 02: Leading a Discussion¶

After your presentation, the group will start talking. This lesson is about what that moment feels like, why it is less intimidating than it sounds, and what you can do to keep things moving, even if you feel out of your depth.

You do not need to lead¶

The word "leading" can make it sound like you need to be in charge of the room. You do not.

In most journal clubs, especially with a mix of junior and senior people, the discussion will carry itself once it gets started. Senior members will ask questions. Others will respond. Your job is much simpler: give the group something to start with, and stay present.

It is completely normal, especially early in your career, to feel like you are not qualified to facilitate a room full of people who know more than you. That feeling does not mean you are doing it wrong. It means you care about doing it well.

Opening the discussion¶

End your presentation with a question or two that you actually find interesting about the paper. It does not need to be sophisticated. For example:

  • "I found this figure surprising. Does it make sense to everyone?"
  • "I am not sure whether I believe the main result. What do you think?"
  • "This reminded me of [other work]. Is that a connection worth making?"

The room will take it from there. If your questions are genuine, if you actually want to know the answer, that shows, and it invites the group to engage honestly.

You are not expected to know all the answers

Once the discussion starts, people will ask questions you cannot answer. That is fine and expected. See the section below.

When you do not know the answer¶

You will be asked things you cannot answer. This is normal for everyone, at every career stage.

A few responses that work:

  • "I am not sure. Does anyone else have a sense of this?"
  • "I did not get to the bottom of that when I was reading. Good question."
  • "I think it might be X, but I am not confident."
  • Simply: "I don't know."

These are all fine. What matters is that you do not guess confidently when you are uncertain. That is the only real mistake.

If a very senior person asks a question you cannot answer, it is still fine to say you do not know. They are not expecting you to know everything; they are engaging with the paper alongside you.

When the room goes quiet¶

Silence after a question is uncomfortable, but it almost always means people are thinking.

Wait longer than feels natural. If the silence persists, try rephrasing: "Put it another way, does the main result here seem convincing?" Or ask someone directly: "What was your reaction to the main figure?"

You do not need to fill silence with words. A few seconds of quiet while people think is not failure.

When the discussion runs away from you¶

Sometimes the group will start debating something you barely understand, or go deep into territory outside your background. This is a success, not a problem. It means the paper sparked something.

You can sit back and let it happen. You can ask a simple question to orient yourself: "Sorry, can someone explain what [term] means in this context?" You do not need to steer every thread back to what you prepared.

The discussion does not have to stay close to your presentation. The presentation was a starting point, not a script.

Closing¶

Most groups have a natural end point: someone checks the time, the room starts to thin out, or the conversation winds down.

A short close is enough:

  • Briefly note what came up. ("Sounds like the main open question is whether this holds at higher energies.")
  • Thank the group.

That is it. You do not need a formal summary or conclusion.

Building confidence over time¶

The first time you lead a discussion will feel harder than the tenth. The discomfort of not knowing everything, of being asked questions you cannot answer, of standing in front of people who know more than you. That fades with repetition.

Every researcher has been in your position. Most of them felt exactly the way you do now, and most of them will not remember the awkward moments you are worried about. What they will remember is whether the discussion was interesting, and that comes from the paper, not from you.

Showing up, presenting honestly, and asking genuine questions is enough. The rest follows from there.
