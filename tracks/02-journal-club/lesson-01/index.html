Lesson 01: How to Present a Paper¶

Journal clubs are one of the most valuable and least intimidating things you will encounter in research. This lesson explains what a journal club actually is, what you are trying to achieve when you present, and how to approach it in a way that is useful to everyone in the room.

What a journal club is¶

A journal club is a regular meeting where one person presents a paper and the group discusses it together.

That is almost the only thing all journal clubs have in common. Every group runs them differently. Some groups use slides; many do not. Some do chalk talks at a whiteboard; others just talk through the paper informally. Presentations can run ten minutes or an hour, depending on the paper and the group. Some groups have a fixed format; others have none at all.

Before your first presentation, find out how your specific group runs things. Ask a senior member what a typical session looks like, whether slides are expected, and how long you have.

What you are actually trying to do¶

The goal of a journal club presentation is not to give a comprehensive review of a paper. It is to start a discussion.

Everything you say should serve that goal. You want to give the group just enough to engage with the paper: what it claims, why it is interesting, and what the key results are. Then get out of the way and let the room talk.

The most successful journal club presentations are not the most polished. They are the ones that get the group arguing, asking questions, and thinking about how the paper connects to their own work.

Why do we care?¶

Every presentation should answer one question first: why did you bring this paper?

The answer does not have to be grand or formally scientific. For example:

  • This is directly relevant to a problem I am working on.
  • I found the result surprising. I would not have expected this.
  • The main figure is striking and I wanted to understand it.
  • I am not sure I believe this result; I want the group's opinion.
  • This is the paper that introduced the technique we all use.
  • I found this confusing and I am hoping someone can explain it to me.

All of these are legitimate reasons to present a paper. The audience does not need to be convinced the paper is important; they need to know why it caught your attention. That is enough to orient the discussion.

What to cover¶

After establishing why you care, the presentation itself is usually three things:

  1. What is the question? What problem is the paper trying to solve, and why does that problem exist?
  2. What did they find? Walk through the key result, usually the main figure or two.
  3. What do you think? Is the result convincing? What would you want to see next? What are you uncertain about?

That is it. Everything else is optional.

You do not need to explain every section of the paper. You do not need to have understood the methods in full detail. Saying "I didn't fully follow section 3, but the key result they get from it is X" is completely fine, and more useful than either skipping over the gap or pretending you understood something you did not.

The main figure is usually enough

Most physics papers have one figure that contains the central result. If you understand what that figure shows and can explain why it matters, you have what you need to run a good discussion. Start there.

Preparing for your talk¶

You do not need to be an expert on the paper to present it. Thorough preparation is useful, but the bar is lower than you probably think.

A reasonable preparation process:

  1. Read the paper properly, at least the abstract, introduction, main results, and conclusion. (See Lesson 02 of the ArXiv track for a structured reading approach.)
  2. Write one or two sentences explaining why you find it interesting.
  3. Identify the key figure, the one that best shows the main result.
  4. Note two or three things you found unclear, surprising, or worth debating.

That is a solid foundation for any journal club presentation. If you have time, read the methods more carefully and look up one or two references. If you do not, the above is enough.

Your first presentation¶

A few things worth knowing before you present for the first time:

It is fine to say "I do not know." Journal club is a shared learning environment. The group is there to think through the paper together. If someone asks a question you cannot answer, say so honestly. "I am not sure, what do you think?" is a perfectly good response, and it moves the discussion forward.

You are not being examined. Presenting at journal club is not a test of how well you understand the paper. It is an invitation to the group to engage with it. If you have misunderstood something, someone will gently correct it, and everyone will learn from that too.

Short is fine. A ten-minute presentation that sparks a lively forty-minute discussion is better than a forty-minute presentation that leaves no time for anyone to respond. When in doubt, cover less and stop sooner.

Your opinion matters. You are not just summarising the paper for people who could read it themselves. Your reaction, what surprised you, what you found confusing, what you are sceptical about, is part of what makes a journal club talk worth attending.

The discussion is the point

A journal club where the presenter talks for 45 minutes and everyone leaves immediately afterwards has not really achieved its purpose. A ten-minute sketch that leads to an hour of engaged discussion has. If you are unsure how long to speak, err shorter.

Using the journal club template¶

If your group uses slides or a structured format, a starting point is available in the Journal Club template. It includes a suggested slide structure and a set of discussion prompts.

Use it as a scaffold, not a script. Adapt it freely to your group's style and the paper you are presenting.
