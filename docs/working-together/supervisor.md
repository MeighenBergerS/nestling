# Working Together: Your Supervisor

Supervision relationships in research take many forms.
The style, frequency of contact, and level of involvement vary enormously between groups,
institutions, and individuals. There is no single template that works for everyone.

That said, some basics tend to hold across the field.
This page names them, not as rules, but as a frame for what to look for and what to expect.

## What supervision typically involves

A research supervisor is responsible for scientific direction, feedback, and professional development.
In practice this usually means:

- Regular meetings with some defined minimum frequency
- Feedback on your written work and presentations
- Guidance on which problems to pursue and how to approach them
- Introductions to the broader community: collaborators, conferences, other groups
- Honest advice about your progress and your career options

These things vary in form. Some supervisors are very hands-on; others give significant independence
from the start. Neither style is inherently wrong. What matters is that you know which one you have
and can work with it.

## Group structure

In many groups, especially large ones, your day-to-day supervision may come from a postdoc or a
senior PhD student rather than the professor directly. This is normal and often beneficial. They are
closer to the work and more accessible. The same expectations apply to this relationship, and the same
issues can arise.

If a problem emerges at this level and cannot be resolved, the appropriate step is to bring it to the
professor or group leader.

## Common failure modes

Supervisors are scientists first. Most were never trained to supervise, and the problems students
experience are common, well-documented, and structural, not personal failures or signs that you have
a uniquely bad situation. You are not alone.

The most frequently reported problems in the field are:

**Availability.** A supervisor who is rarely reachable, cancels meetings repeatedly, or goes weeks
without meaningful contact leaves students without direction. This is the single most common complaint.

**Unclear expectations.** Students often do not know what their thesis needs to contain, what "done"
looks like, or how close they are to finishing. If this is never stated explicitly, ask directly. It
is a reasonable question and you are entitled to a real answer.

**Slow feedback on written work.** Drafts can sit for weeks. This is common enough that it is worth
agreeing on a turnaround expectation early, before you submit a draft, not after.

**Career guidance.** Supervisors teach the science but often do not give honest guidance on careers:
job market realities, networking, how to position yourself. This gap is common. If it is not coming
from your supervisor, seek other sources: senior postdocs, other faculty, professional societies.

**Credit and visibility.** Your contributions should be visible. This includes authorship on papers,
being sent to give talks, and being introduced to collaborators. Consistent invisibility is worth
raising.

**Service work.** In large experimental collaborations, service work, such as detector shifts,
software maintenance, and computing tasks, can consume a student's time without contributing to their
thesis. A good supervisor protects their students from being over-assigned to this. If it is happening
to you, it is a legitimate thing to raise.

**The two extremes.** Some supervisors micromanage; others disengage after the first year once a
student can work independently. Both are problems. The goal is a relationship that gives increasing
autonomy over time while remaining genuinely engaged.

**Feedback culture.** Harsh, dismissive, or belittling feedback is not a sign of high standards. It
is a failure of supervision. Critical feedback delivered with respect is normal and expected; feedback
that makes you feel worthless is not.

**Power imbalance.** Your supervisor writes your recommendation letters. This dynamic is real, and it
is one of the main reasons students stay silent about problems. Knowing that this pressure exists is
the first step to not being controlled by it.

## Institution policy is the overriding authority

Your university or research institution has formal policies on supervision, grievances, and student
welfare. These take precedence over any informal arrangement or group culture, including anything
written here. Know where to find them.

## The key signal

If something is wrong, the right first step is almost always to raise it with your supervisor directly.
Most problems can be resolved this way, and most supervisors respond well to a student who comes to
them honestly.

**If raising it with your supervisor does not feel possible, that is when there is an actual
problem.** At that point, the right move is to escalate: the graduate coordinator, a second supervisor,
the department ombudsperson, or another trusted faculty member. You should not have to manage a
broken supervision relationship alone. The structures to help you exist. Find out what they are at
your institution before you need them.
